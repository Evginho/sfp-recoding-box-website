## The Short Version

Every SFP module has a small memory chip (EEPROM) that stores its identity — vendor name, part number, serial number, and diagnostic calibration. Switches read this data when a module is inserted. Some switches check the vendor name against an allow-list and reject unknown modules.

**SFP recoding = changing that identity data** so the module passes the check.

## What You Can Change

- **Vendor name** (bytes 20–35): e.g. `GENERIC SFP+    ` → `CISCO-SFP       `
- **Part number** (bytes 40–55): e.g. `10G-SR-GENERIC  ` → `SFP-10G-SR      `
- **Serial number** (bytes 76–83): for asset tracking or OEM builds
- **Date code** (bytes 84–91)
- **DDM thresholds** (high memory, A2h page): temperature, power, bias alarms

## What You Can't Change

- **Optical characteristics** — the module still has whatever laser/receiver it was built with. Recoding changes the label, not the hardware.
- **Physical form factor** — you can't make an SFP look like an SFP+ in electrical testing (though most equipment doesn't check this strictly)
- **Write-protected EEPROMs** — some OEM modules set a hardware write-protect bit. You can still read them.

## Is It Ethical?

That depends on what you're doing:

- **Lab testing and learning**: ✅ 100% fine
- **Saving money on modules for your own equipment**: ✅ Generally fine; check your service contracts
- **Deceiving a vendor's support process**: ❌ Probably violates your service agreement

The tool is neutral. How you use it is your responsibility.
